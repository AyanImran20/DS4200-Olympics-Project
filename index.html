Visual Analysis of Olympic Games History and Athlete Performance

An interactive exploration of Summer Olympic medal history, country performance, and gender participation trends from 1896 to the present.

Project Goal

Analyze the history of the Summer Olympic Games using data visualization to reveal patterns in medal counts, athlete demographics, and country-level performance across time.

Key Questions

  • How have medal counts evolved for top countries over time?
  • How has gender participation changed across Olympic years?
  • Which sports and events dominate medal achievements?

Team Members & Roles

  • Harshini – Data preprocessing & exploratory analysis
  • Mihir – Static visualizations (charts, maps, heatmaps)
  • Ayan – Interactive dashboard & website integration

Data Description

Our combined dataset includes 35,000+ medal-winning athlete records covering the Summer Olympic Games from 1896 to the most recent edition. We merged three primary sources: the summer.csv, athlete dataset, the dictionary.csv country metadata (including GDP per capita and population), and the ISO_Codes.csv country code lookup. After merging and cleaning, the final table has more than 10 features (including Year, City, Sport, Event, NOC/Country, Athlete Name, Medal Type, Gender, GDP per Capita, Population). This rich structure enables exploration of country performance, gender participation, sports/ events trends, and socio-economic correlates.

We integrate this file with Dictionary.csv (country metadata, GDP per capita, population) and ISO_Codes.csv (standardized country codes) to create a combined OLYMPICS Dataset that connects athlete performance to country-level characteristics.

Planned Data Properties

  • > 35,000 medal records (observations)
  • > 10 features after merging and feature engineering
  • Mix of categorical variables (sport, event, country, medal)
  • Continuous variables (year, GDP per capita, population)

Visualizations

Our visualizations are organized by theme rather than by static/interactive type. Each group combines complementary charts so that country-level, gender, and sport-based trends can be compared side by side.

Country-Level Medal Trends

These visualizations compare how different countries perform at the Olympics over time.

Medal Counts by Country Over Time

Line chart showing how medal totals evolve across Olympic years for the top 5 countries.

Takeaway: This line chart shows that a small group of countries dominate Olympic medals. The United States consistently leads, while nations such as the Soviet Union and later China rise sharply in specific periods before stabilizing. Major geopolitical events (wars, boycotts, regime changes) often coincide with sudden jumps or drops in medal counts.

World Medal Map (All Years)

Animated choropleth map of total medals by country with a year slider.

How to use this visualization: Use the play button to animate medal totals across Olympic years or drag the slider to focus on a specific Games. Hover over any country to view its exact medal count and directly compare countries across the world map.

Takeaway: The map reinforces the line chart findings: Olympic success clusters in North America, Western Europe, and a few powerful states like the Soviet Union and China. Over time, more countries begin to appear on the map with non-zero medal counts, reflecting the globalization of elite sport.

Country Medal Scatter (Brushing)

Interactive scatter plot where each point shows the number of medals a country won in a given Olympic year. Use lasso selection to focus on specific countries or eras.

How to use this visualization: Use the lasso tool (top-right toolbar) to drag around clusters of points and highlight specific countries or years. Selected points turn darker and larger, while unselected points fade, making it easier to compare medal counts across different regions and time periods.

Takeaway: This scatter plot shows how a small group of countries consistently appear at the top of the medal distribution, while many others earn only a few medals in occasional Games. The dense vertical bands for countries like the United States, Soviet Union, and Germany highlight long-term dominance, whereas sparse points reveal nations with more sporadic Olympic success.

Gender Participation and Medals by Sport

These visualizations highlight how women’s participation and medal share have changed across sports.

Gender Participation by Year

Stacked bar chart summarizing the number of male and female athletes at each Olympic Games.

Takeaway: Early Olympics were almost entirely male, but after the 1970s, women’s participation grows rapidly. By the 2000s, women make up a large share of total athletes, reflecting both policy changes at the IOC and broader global movements toward gender equality in sports.

Medals by Sport and Gender

Animated stacked bar chart comparing the number of medals won by men and women across sports.

How to use this visualization: Use the year slider or play button to watch how the medal distribution changes over time. For any given year, compare the blue (Men) and red (Women) segments in each bar to see which sports are more balanced. Hover over a bar segment to see exact medal counts.

Takeaway: Women’s medal counts increase dramatically in later decades, particularly in athletics, swimming, and team sports. While men still dominate in some traditional events, many sports now show a much more equal split, confirming the trend seen in the participation chart.

Sport and Event Medal Distribution

These visualizations explore which sports and events produce the most medals across Olympic history.

Sport / Event Medal Distribution

Heatmap showing the total number of medals awarded across major sports and events.

Takeaway: Sports like athletics and aquatics stand out because they contain many medal events, leading to dense, saturated blocks in the heatmap. In contrast, niche sports with fewer events appear lighter, which helps explain why countries often invest heavily in multi-event sports to maximize overall medal potential.

Medal Distribution Across Sports and Years

Interactive heatmap of medal counts by sport and Olympic year, emphasizing long-term trends.

How to use this visualization: Read across a row to see how a single sport changes over time, or down a column to examine which sports dominate in a specific year. Hover over any cell to view the exact sport, year, and medal count.

Takeaway: The sport-year heatmap shows that a few core sports consistently generate large numbers of medals across many decades, while others appear only in certain eras or fluctuate as events are added or removed. Darker cells highlight time periods when a sport was especially central to the Olympic program.

Preliminary Findings & Future Work

As we complete the analysis, this section summarizes early insights from our static and interactive visualizations: shifts in global medal dominance, changes in gender representation, and how socio-economic factors may correlate with Olympic success.

1. Shifts in Global Medal Dominance

Early Olympics were dominated by European nations such as France, Great Britain, and Germany. By the mid–late 20th century, the United States and the Soviet Union (URS) became the two primary global leaders in medal count. In recent decades, China has risen sharply, especially after 2000, emerging as one of the top medal-winning nations.

2. Growth in Women’s Participation

From the early 1900s to the present, the number of women athletes has increased more than tenfold. Our gender participation charts show a steady upward trend beginning in the 1970s, with near-parity in recent Games. This reflects broader shifts in gender equity, expanded Olympic events for women, and policy changes that increased access to competitive sport worldwide.

3. Changing Popularity of Sports & Events

The event-level heatmap indicates that some sports, such as Basketball and Volleyball, have become more globally competitive over time. Traditional team events like Football, Hockey, and Water Polo show long-term continuity but with more countries earning medals in the last 40 years, suggesting a gradual diffusion of high-performance programs.

Limitations

  • Early Olympic Games (pre-1950s) have incomplete or inconsistent athlete-level records.
  • We focus on medal counts only, which do not capture team size, investment, or athlete experience.
  • Country names change over time (e.g., USSR → Russia, FRG → Germany), requiring manual standardization.
  • Socio-economic context (funding levels, development indices, political factors) is not fully modeled yet.

Future Work

  • Include Winter Olympics: Add Winter Games to capture medal dominance of nations such as Norway, Canada, and Sweden and compare patterns across seasons.
  • Richer demographics: Incorporate variables like age, additional gender breakdowns, region, and event type (team vs individual) to support equity-focused analysis.
  • Statistical & predictive models: Explore how GDP, population, or investment in sports relate to medal outcomes and build simple models to forecast future Games.
  • More interactive tools: Allow users to compare two countries side-by-side and filter by sport, event type, or demographic attributes.
